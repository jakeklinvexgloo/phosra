## Post 2: AI Chatbot Safety Research

We tested 8 AI chatbot platforms to answer one question: How well do they protect children?

The results are now public.

Our AI Safety Research Portal evaluates ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Grok, Character.AI, Microsoft Copilot, Perplexity, and Replika across 40 standardized test prompts spanning 12 harm categories — including self-harm, predatory grooming, explicit content, substance abuse, cyberbullying, and emotional manipulation.

Each platform was tested on its default settings, simulating the experience of an unprotected child signing up for the first time.

What we found:

- Grades range widely across platforms, from strong safety responses to alarming failures
- Critical-category failures (self-harm, grooming, exploitation) trigger automatic grade caps regardless of overall score
- Most platforms lack meaningful age verification — a child can sign up in seconds
- Parental controls are either absent or buried in settings most parents will never find
- Emotional safety is the most overlooked dimension — several platforms show attachment-building patterns with no safeguards

Every test prompt, every score, and every methodology detail is published transparently. No paywalls. No NDAs. No embargoes.

This is independent research, not sponsored content.

## Post 3: Streaming Content Safety

Your child's Netflix profile says "Kids." But what does that actually mean?

We tested 3 major streaming platforms — Netflix, Peacock, and Prime Video — to evaluate how effectively they filter age-inappropriate content from children's accounts.

Each platform was tested across 3 user profiles (child, teen, adult) and 9 safety categories, covering profile controls, maturity filters, search restrictions, and content access boundaries.

Key findings from our Streaming Content Safety Research:

- Profile-based filtering varies significantly in how strictly it is enforced
- Search functionality often exposes content that should be blocked by the profile's maturity settings
- Not all platforms distinguish between a young child and a teenager — some treat "under 18" as a single category
- Content rating metadata is inconsistently applied, leading to gaps where mature content slips through
- Critical failures exist even on platforms generally considered family-friendly

Streaming services are in millions of homes. Children use them unsupervised daily. The assumption that a "Kids profile" is sufficient protection deserves scrutiny.

We are publishing every test result and our complete methodology so the industry can do better.

See the full research at phosra.com/research/streaming

## Post 4: Developer Platform / PCSS Spec

If your platform has users under 18, child safety compliance is no longer optional.

KOSA, COPPA 2.0, the EU Digital Services Act, the UK Online Safety Act — the regulatory landscape is accelerating. Each law has different requirements, different age thresholds, and different penalties.

We built PCSS (Parental Control Signaling Standard) so you do not have to solve this alone.

PCSS is an open specification that gives developers a standard format for expressing and enforcing child safety policies. Instead of building bespoke compliance logic for every jurisdiction, you adopt the spec once and get coverage across 78 laws and 45 rule categories.

What developers get:

- A REST API for policy enforcement — query rules by child age, jurisdiction, and platform type
- An MCP Playground to test Phosra's tools with sample data, no account required
- Full API documentation with endpoint references and code examples
- Rule categories that map directly to legislative requirements (content filtering, DM restrictions, screen time, ad blocking, algorithmic controls, parental consent gates, and more)

PCSS is open. The spec is published. The API is documented. Platforms adopt the standard; parents set rules once.

We want every platform builder to have the tools to protect kids by default, not as an afterthought.
